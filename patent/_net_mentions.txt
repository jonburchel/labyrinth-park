L715: ### 23. Tile Mesh Network Architecture
L717: **23.1 Power Mesh.** The aggregated inter-tile DC couplings and trough-fed power contacts form a meshed 48V DC power distribution network. Each tile carries a PMU with (i) input arbitration across up to seven sources (one trough plus up to six neighbors for hex, or one plus four for square), (ii) local energy storage (a small lithium iron phosphate buffer, 100 to 500 Wh) that rides through source transitions, and (iii) a soft-start output to the tile's loads. The central controller, aware of instantaneous load and source topology, computes power-flow solutions that respect conductor ampacity on each link, schedules deployments that would otherwise cause overload, and routes power around weak or failed links.
L719: **23.2 Data and Fiber Mesh.** Each tile hosts a managed Layer-2/Layer-3 switch/router node with at least one port per edge (Ethernet and/or fiber) and one internal port to the tile payload, plus an integrated dual-band WiFi 6/6E access point and Bluetooth Low Energy beacon. Tiles participate in a property-wide mesh running IEEE 802.1s/802.1aq or a comparable redundant L2 protocol, or a routed L3 overlay (for example, OSPF or BGP EVPN) with sub-second reconvergence. The combined tile mesh provides a distributed outdoor wireless fabric, seamless roaming, and redundant backhaul. Expected single-link capacity is 1 Gb/s Ethernet and up to 10 or 25 Gb/s over fiber, with aggregate property bandwidth on the order of tens to hundreds of Gb/s depending on topology.
L721: **23.3 Water and Hydraulic Mesh.** Inter-tile water couplings and sub-channel stubs form a meshed hydraulic network. Each tile includes a supply valve, a return valve, a pressure sensor, and a flow meter. The controller treats the mesh as a directed graph of pipe segments with known diameters and lengths, solves flow distribution to active consumers (fountains, misters, bar sinks, irrigation), and reserves headroom for irrigation demand. Segment isolation permits leak containment to a single tile or link.
L723: **23.4 Self-Healing and Rerouting.** When a tile is removed, fails, or loses an edge connection, the controller (i) detects the topology change through link-state beacons on the data mesh and flow/voltage/pressure changes on the power and water meshes, (ii) recomputes routing for power, data, and water, (iii) commands neighboring tiles to carry redirected load within their rated limits, and (iv) alerts operators if any connected load becomes unserviceable. Critical loads (emergency lighting, safety sensors, egress audio) are pre-assigned at least two disjoint paths.
L727: **23.6 Capacity Planning.** Representative sizing: for a 400-tile property with 10 kW peak demand per tile (inverter plus lighting plus audio plus utility access) and 48V backbone, each manifold supports 50 A continuous (2.4 kW), and the controller distributes load so that no single manifold exceeds 80 percent rated current. Data capacity is provisioned for worst-case concurrent 4K video streams on AV tiles and at least 100 simultaneous guest devices per access point. Water capacity is provisioned for the aggregate flow of the largest expected event (fountains plus bar sinks plus mist cooling plus irrigation makeup).
L733: **24.2 Site Fitting and Inventory Allocation.** The orchestration service solves for a geometric placement of the template in the target area, selects specific physical tiles from the property inventory subject to availability, payload type, health, and service status, and assigns each selected tile to a specific destination trough position. The solver respects horticultural constraints (hedge tiles not left in sun-stress positions longer than their species limit), acoustic constraints (audio tile spacing and aim), utility constraints (sufficient power, water, and data on the mesh path to each destination), and ADA constraints (accessible aisle widths, firm stable surfaces, ramps at terrace boundaries).
L737: **24.4 Interconnection Verification.** After physical positioning, the controller commands manifold engagement at each shared edge, verifies power, data, and water continuity across each expected link, runs a self-test on each payload (audio swept-tone check, lighting DMX roll call, fountain pump prime, GFCI test on pop-up outlets), and produces a signed readiness report. Any failed link or payload triggers automatic remediation (retry engagement, swap in a reserve tile, or reroute utilities through an alternate path) before releasing the venue to operators.
L741: **24.6 Post-Event Teardown.** On event completion, the controller reverses the orchestration: payloads are unpowered in a controlled sequence, manifolds disengage, tiles return to storage or default positions via the tunnel network where available, pop-up utilities retract, and the site returns to its default configuration (for example, a uniform lawn). The system records utilization metrics (power, water, runtime, wear) against each tile and updates preventive-maintenance schedules.

Per-hop latency is less than 1 millisecond; a worst-case 20-hop path introduces less than 20 milliseconds of latency, acceptable for all real-time applications including audio synchronization and camera streaming. At full utilization with all 682 tiles streaming 1080p video simultaneously (approximately 5 Mbps per stream), total aggregate bandwidth is approximately 3.4 Gbps, well within the fiber backbone capacity. Fiber uplinks to the property backbone are provided every 20 to 30 tiles for redundancy.

L983: A universal modular landscape infrastructure platform uses mobile tile bases mounted on motorized platforms within a standardized recessed channel grid. In the preferred embodiment, each platform employs a trough-and-elevator mechanism: a receiving trough set into the path surface receives the container flush with grade, and an elevator raises the container approximately 5 inches to expose a roller assembly for autonomous transit to a new trough position. The invention further comprises a universal trough grid extending across the entire property, enabling maze sections to be placed at any grid point and to migrate across the landscape seasonally or permanently. When trough sections are unoccupied, modular surface covers conceal the infrastructure, presenting as lawn, garden, or paved surfaces. The elevator mechanism is itself a modular, relocatable unit that can be installed in or removed from recessed bays at any trough position, making the locations of active transit junctions reconfigurable. Each standardized planter uses an inert lightweight growing medium with a wicking interface that draws water and nutrients from sub-channels integrated into the trough infrastructure, forming a closed-loop hydroponic irrigation system that reduces container weight 40-60% versus soil. The recirculating irrigation network is divided into independently isolatable hydraulic zones with pathogen detection, backflow prevention, and disinfection stages enabling automatic quarantine of affected planters or zones. Each planter includes a root-management assembly comprising air-pruning surfaces, guided-root ribs, or a replaceable inner root cartridge, with the control system assigning root-service intervals and autonomously routing planters to service bays. Adjacent planters include canopy-separation features and root-bridging prevention barriers. The system may be implemented at vehicular scale with vehicle-rated corridors, hedge walls of 8-12 feet, and grade-separated bridge/underpass crossings, creating a reconfigurable driving maze that presents a different route on every visit. Each platform includes a self-leveling suspension system compensating for outdoor surface irregularities, redundant fail-safe braking, wind-load stabilization, freeze-thaw protection, and degraded-mobility support for wheel or suspension failures. A central control system commands positions according to algorithmic scheduling, validates safety and egress compliance, and maintains a digital twin for simulation. The system includes a hospitality quiet-hours mode that sequences unit movement according to acoustic thresholds and guest-room proximity, a responder-navigation subsystem with addressable waypoints and incident-control logic for emergency medical extraction, and multiple simultaneously valid guest-route classes including wheelchair-optimized, stroller-optimized, low-sensory, and restroom-proximate routes. The system integrates with a guest navigation application providing progressive-reveal wayfinding, an emergency access mode for rapid egress, a section rotation system for operational flexibility, utility docking manifolds for automated irrigation and charging, plant-health monitoring with nursery rotation and seasonal conditioning, anti-aerial-observation features, and severe-weather management. The mobile container is generalized as a universal landscape tile with a standardized mobile base and an interchangeable surface payload selected from a tile family including lawn, hedge/planter, furniture, event surface, decorative, utility, lighting, audio/AV, bar/service, fire/water-feature, sensor/environmental, and emergency-response tiles. Each tile base carries an inter-tile connectivity manifold on each external edge, providing self-aligning 48V DC power couplings, fiber and Ethernet data couplings, and water supply and return quick-connects, enabling adjacent tiles to exchange power, data, and water without external cabling. The aggregated inter-tile couplings form a self-healing meshed network for power, data, and water, with each tile hosting a managed network node, dual-band wireless access point, and power management unit that arbitrates multiple sources.

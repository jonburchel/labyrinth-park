Invention Summary

The Universal Modular Landscape Infrastructure Platform is a system of interlocking, robotically repositionable landscape tiles that enable a living outdoor environment to autonomously reconfigure itself. Each tile is a self-contained module integrating structural support, planting substrate, integrated irrigation, embedded power distribution, and sensor instrumentation. Tiles are transported on a sub-grade rail network using powered roller assemblies, allowing the physical layout of a garden, maze, or landscape installation to change configuration without human labor or heavy equipment.

The platform introduces a three-part architecture separating the permanent sub-grade infrastructure (drainage, power, data backbone) from the relocatable tile layer and the living horticultural layer above. This separation allows each layer to be maintained, upgraded, or replaced independently. A central software control system orchestrates tile movements, irrigation scheduling, plant health monitoring, and guest experience programming through a unified API, enabling what the application terms a "software-defined landscape."

The system is designed for commercial deployment in hospitality, theme park, botanical garden, and high-end residential contexts. It addresses longstanding limitations in landscape architecture where physical layouts are essentially permanent once installed, creating a new category of infrastructure that combines the permanence of hardscape with the adaptability of software-controlled robotics.

Recommended Next Steps for Attorney Review

1. Claim 1 Breadth and Prior Art

Multiple expert reviewers (patent-law-critical, lit-curious, ptab-critical) flagged potential anticipation of Claim 1 by Harvest Automation's autonomous plant-moving robots. The current Claim 1 may be too broad without explicit structural limitations distinguishing from known modular paving or agricultural automation systems.

Recommendation: Consider pulling trough-concealment limitations (the sub-grade channel architecture that hides utilities beneath tile surfaces) into Claim 1 for the non-provisional filing. This narrows the claim but creates a much stronger position against Harvest Automation and similar prior art.

2. Crown Jewel Claims

PTAB reviewers identified the following claims as the strongest and most defensible:

  • Claims 78-82: Three-part architecture (sub-grade / tile / horticultural layer separation). These claims capture the core inventive concept and are well-differentiated from known systems.
  • Claim 86: Software-defined landscape. This claim covers the API-driven orchestration layer that treats a physical landscape as a programmable resource. Reviewers noted this has no meaningful prior art analog.

These claims should receive priority attention in the non-provisional and may warrant additional dependent claims to build a stronger family around them.

3. Species List Review (Section 2.2)

The horticultural experts (hort-critical, hort-curious) raised concerns about the species list in Section 2.2. Several listed species may have long-term viability issues in shallow-substrate container environments. A qualified horticulturist should review the species selections to ensure they are defensible as actually workable embodiments, not merely aspirational.

4. Abstract Length

The current abstract exceeds the USPTO limit for non-provisional applications. It needs trimming to 150 words or fewer for the non-provisional filing.

5. Continuation and Divisional Strategy

Given the breadth of claim categories (apparatus, method, software, platform), counsel should consider whether a divisional filing strategy is warranted to protect each category in a separate application, reducing restriction risk.

Important Notes

Dollar Figures

All dollar figures appearing in the patent specification (cost estimates, market projections, pricing examples) are marked as "illustrative" throughout the document. This was done per expert recommendation to avoid any argument that specific dollar amounts constitute claim limitations or admissions.

Water Architecture Alternatives

The patent describes two distinct water distribution architectures as alternatives, both of which are independently claimed:

  1. Sub-channel architecture: Water flows through channels integrated into the sub-grade rail system beneath the tiles
  2. Inter-tile manifold architecture: Water is distributed through quick-connect manifolds at tile boundary interfaces

Both architectures are presented as alternative embodiments. Neither is described as preferred, ensuring the claims cover installations using either approach.
